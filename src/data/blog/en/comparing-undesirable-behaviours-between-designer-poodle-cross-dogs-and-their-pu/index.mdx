Designer-crossbreed dogs like Cockapoos, Labradoodles, and Cavapoos have surged in popularity, often touted as hypoallergenic, easy to train, and great with kids. But do these designer mixes really live up to their reputations? A large-scale UK study analyzed thousands of owner reports to compare the behavior of these designer-crossbreeds with their purebred parent breeds, revealing some surprising insights about their temperaments.

> **TL;DR**
> - Designer-crossbreed dogs often show more undesirable behaviors than one or both of their purebred progenitor breeds, especially Cockapoos and Cavapoos.
> - Labradoodles show a more mixed behavioral profile, sometimes scoring better and sometimes worse than their purebred parents, suggesting that each designer-crossbreed is behaviorally unique.

Over the past decade, designer-crossbreeds—dogs bred by crossing two or more pure breeds—have become increasingly popular, particularly Poodle mixes like the Cockapoo (Cocker Spaniel x Poodle), Labradoodle (Labrador Retriever x Poodle), and Cavapoo (Cavalier King Charles Spaniel x Poodle). Many owners choose these dogs based on the belief that they are easier to train, healthier, and better suited for families with children. However, despite their popularity, there has been limited scientific evidence comparing the behavior of these designer-crossbreeds to their purebred ancestors. This gap leaves prospective dog owners relying on anecdotes rather than data when making important decisions about which dog to bring home.

To address this gap, researchers collected behavioral data from over 9,400 UK dog owners through an online survey conducted in early 2023. The survey used the Canine Behavioural Assessment and Research Questionnaire (C-BARQ), a validated tool that measures 12 different behavioral traits ranging from aggression to excitability. The study focused on dogs acquired as puppies (16 weeks or younger) between 2019 and 2023 to reduce age-related biases. Owners of Cockapoos, Labradoodles, Cavapoos, and their purebred progenitor breeds (Cocker Spaniel, Labrador Retriever, Cavalier King Charles Spaniel, and various Poodle types) reported on their dogs’ behaviors. The researchers then used statistical modeling to compare undesirable behaviors across the designer-crossbreeds and their parent breeds.

Contrary to popular belief, the study found that designer-crossbreeds often exhibited more undesirable behaviors than their purebred parents. Across 72 behavioral comparisons, designer-crossbreeds scored worse in 44.4% of cases and better in only 9.7%. Cockapoos showed the most undesirable behaviors, scoring worse than both Cocker Spaniels and Poodles in 16 out of 24 behavioral traits assessed. Cavapoos also scored worse than both progenitors in 11 out of 12 differing traits. Labradoodles, however, presented a more balanced profile, scoring worse in five behaviors but better in six compared to their purebred parents. These results suggest that behavioral traits in designer-crossbreeds do not simply average out between parent breeds but can vary significantly depending on the specific cross.

These findings have important implications for prospective dog owners and animal welfare. The widespread assumption that designer-crossbreeds are inherently easier to train or more suitable for families with children is not consistently supported by evidence. Misguided expectations can lead to challenges in dog ownership, increased risks such as dog bites, and even relinquishment of dogs when behaviors do not meet owner expectations. By providing a clearer picture of the behavioral tendencies of popular designer-crossbreeds, this study helps prospective owners make more informed decisions and encourages realistic expectations about training and socialization needs.

While the study benefits from a large sample size and a validated behavioral questionnaire, it relies on owner-reported data, which can be influenced by subjective perceptions and reporting biases. Additionally, environmental factors such as training methods, owner experience, and household dynamics were not fully controlled and can affect dog behavior. The study also focused on dogs under five years old in the UK, so findings may not generalize to older dogs or those in other countries. Further research incorporating direct behavioral assessments and longitudinal follow-up would help deepen understanding of how designer-crossbreeds develop behaviorally over time.
